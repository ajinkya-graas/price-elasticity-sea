ℹ️  What is this? How does it work?

▼

This tool measures price elasticity of demand — how much quantity sold changes when a product's price changes. It helps answer questions like: If we discount this SKU by 10%, will the volume increase enough to offset the margin hit? Are customers more price-sensitive during a 11.11 sale vs. a regular day? Which categories respond most to price changes on TikTok vs. Shopee?

Step 1
Collect daily data
Order-level data (SKU, channel, price, qty) from Jan 2024 across 6 SEA markets, enriched with product catalogue (Gender, Division, RBU, Colour).
Step 2
Classify day types
D-day = mega campaigns (3.3–12.12) · Special = mid-month (14–15) & payday (last 4 days) · BAU = everything else.
Step 3
Log-log regression
For each SKU we fit an OLS regression on log(price) vs. log(quantity). The slope is the elasticity coefficient. Requires ≥5 distinct price points.
Step 4
Filter & compare
Slice by channel, gender, division, RBU, colour code, day type, and date range. All charts recalculate live — no server needed.

How to read the elasticity number

Below −1 · Elastic
Demand drops more than proportionally when price rises. Customers are price-sensitive — discounting drives significant volume uplift.
−1 to 0 · Inelastic
Demand holds up even as price changes. Brand loyalty or lack of alternatives. You have pricing power here.
Positive · Anomalous
Higher price correlates with more demand — possible for luxury/status goods, or a data artefact (e.g. bundles). Interpret with caution.
Data coverage: Top 500 SKUs per market by volume · Free / zero-price units counted in volume stats but excluded from elasticity · ~94% of sales volume has full product metadata · ~6% (~10,600 SKUs) not matched to catalogue — see unmapped_skus.csv.
